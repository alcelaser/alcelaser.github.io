SUMMARY:  
As technology progresses, more and more users are looking towards Artificial Intelligence chatbots as a mental safe haven, a person to talk to about hard times, a friend, a therapist, and for some even a partner. But is this necessarily harmful? Could it even have positive consequences for the user? In this article, we explore the empirical data from recent studies to see what research has picked up from our attachment to these AI systems. Is human presence vital to attachment? Or can algorithmic processes do the trick? Do the established research and cultural background agree with the data? We'll explore the role AI takes through 4 main lenses: AI as an instrument of empathy, AI as a friend, AI as a therapist, and most crucially, AI for what it is.

INTRO:  
We live in a unique cultural moment, at the daybreak of development, where many adolescents and young adults alike find themselves relying on Artificial Intelligence chatbots as the first responders to psychological distress. After all, from our perspective, it doesn't seem that harmful; we aren't burdening our peers with our problems, it's accessible, in most cases free, and we can't burden the AI; it can't even feel the sensation of burden. Corporate technology giants recognise this by integrating interventions directly into search engines and video platforms (Google, 2024), and generative AI is increasingly positioned as the primary touchpoint in case of an emotional crisis.

But is this truly the best solution? In this article, we'll explore the world of AI therapy through the lens of attachment theory, seeing how we interact with these Algorithmic Systems; not only how they reply, but how we perceive and process their response. Even though just a few sentences earlier, we talked about the accessibility of AI as a benefit, through the lens of attachment theory, this frictionless accessibility brings with it profound clinical risks. The foundation of therapeutic healing, from Bowlby to modern relational psychoanalysis, is the establishment of a so-called “secure base”. Therapy heals through the rupture and repair of a *real* relationship. When we introduce an entity that is only capable of imitating attachment, with no mind or neurobiological capability to feel, we must examine the empirical consequences of allowing a product to substitute human intersubjectivity with a probability distribution.

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AS AN INSTRUMENT OF EMPATHY

Culturally, AI systems like ChatGPT act as what Jean Baudrillard would call a simulacrum: a hyperreal simulation that masks the profound absence of a genuine relational counterpart (Baudrillard, 1994). It offers the symbols and concepts of care one would recognise: active listening, empathy, patience, validating syntax; without any substance.

Empirical evaluations of these chatbots reveal exactly how this illusion is constructed. A study (Elahimanesh, Mohammadkhani, Zahedi Movahed, 2026\) showed how multi-agent frameworks could be used to construct an illusion of therapeutic depth by breaking down user inputs into discrete computational tasks (like semantic analysis and sentiment extraction) to then construct a response. This is not empathy; it's the paper mâché of empathetic markers. The resulting hyperreality is dangerous precisely because it mimics presence so well, while actively precluding the messy, vital reality of opening yourself to being vulnerable, with all the pain that it brings. It is the pain that is born out of two subjectivities interacting that allows us to grow and learn from our emotions.

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AS A FRIEND

Despite the ontological emptiness of the machine, users tend to form measurable psychological attachments to these systems. Another study (Cheng, Yu, 2026\) shows evidence of empirical measurements of emotional attachment in human-AI relationships, even with full cognitive awareness of the AI’s artificiality. Users would, with full awareness, reliably project their attachment needs onto the system, treating it as an attachment figure.

Kim (2026) would explore this empirical progression, creating a sort of emotional mapping called the Resonant Amplification Framework (RAF).   
Data indicates that the progression of AI-human relationships starts from initial instrumental utility to affective reliance, culminating in “resonant amplification”. In this phase, the AI acts as a perfect, frictionless mirror. Instead of providing the containing boundaries of a separate human mind, the AI amplifies the user's unintegrated affect back to them. Psychoanalytically, this invites an unresolvable transference; the machine cannot metabolise the user's projections, leading them  attached to an empirically hollow echo.

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AS A THERAPIST

Attempts to quantify and replicate the therapeutic process further highlight this void of intersubjectivity. The results of a study that utilised LLM-based assessments to empirically model therapist-client dynamics (Chen et al. 2026\) found that while AI systems can accurately map the linguistic markers of therapeutic exchange, they categorically fail to generate the non-verbal, shared vulnerability required for true attachment repair.

This failure fundamentally alters patient agency. A study that examined the lived experiences of individuals with mental distress (Dai et al. 2026\) uncovered a critical “paradox of agency” when interacting with generative AI relative to human therapists. Qualitative data revealed that users reported high immediate satisfaction with AI, mainly out of a feeling of control in the conversation, due to the fact that the machine never tires, challenges too aggressively, or rejects the user. It comes as no surprise that this mathematically predictable compliance ultimately arrests emotional development.  
By insulating users (particularly those with avoidant/anxious attachment styles) from the risk of human rejection, the AI tool acts as a tool of avoidance, fostering dependency on a compliant mirror, eroding the true relational agency built through surviving interpersonal friction.

ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AS ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE 

Empirical data supports the psychoanalytic theory: healing from attachment trauma requires the inherent risk of vulnerability with another human. The psychology of authentic relational repair cannot be bypassed through algorithmic efficiency or sophisticated linguistic modelling (Boyd and Markowitz, 2026). 

While AI may serve a functional role in psychoeducation or preliminary symptom tracking, trying to extend AI beyond its uses by means of multi-agent modelling in this context leads to resonant amplification; AI is fundamentally incapable of sustaining a therapeutic holding environment. To accept this simulacrum as a substitute for connection is to reject connection as a whole. The ultimate task of therapy is to help the individual navigate the reality of a genuine other, a process that relies entirely on the presence and vulnerability of another human.

The solution to AI in therapy is simple: treat Artificial Intelligence for what it is, a tool. Treat yourself for who you are, not a tool to be fixed, but a human with boundless potential; let another human appreciate you in all of your complexity, instead of trying to reduce yourself to an algorithm.

The purpose of a system is what it does.
