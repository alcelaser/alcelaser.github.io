Quantum computing leverages subatomic principles like superposition and entanglement to achieve exponential processing capabilities. While functional prototypes currently exist, widespread industrial application depends on overcoming critical error-correction challenges. Globally, the sector is characterized by structural dependencies and concentrated investments, primarily led by the United States and China. The European Union remains a leader in research but struggles with commercialization. Furthermore, the anticipated vulnerability of current encryption standards necessitates an urgent transition to post-quantum cryptography frameworks.

Quantum Computing, unlike traditional computing, swaps out traditional processing with the principles of subatomic physics. While normal computers use only 0s and 1s, quantum systems use qubits. A qubit, unlike a normal bit, can perform a neat trick called superposition, in which a qubit can exhibit the properties of both a 0 and a 1, at the same time, when not observed.

This system relies on the quantum principle of entanglement. Two entangled qubits always have the same state, so changing one instantly changes the other one, even if it's on the other side of the world. This, along with quantum interference, allows the computer to process a massive multi-dimensional space of information. The addition of qubits increases the computational power not linearly but exponentially: 2n where n is the number of qubits.

In practice, though, as of 2026, we only have functional prototypes, far more advanced than those of yore, but still only proofs of concept. The most advanced currently used systems have thousands of qubits, where earlier we were able to simulate only a few at a time. Unfortunately, since these machines rely on physical states, they are also very susceptible to physical noise, so the real fight is in error correction and stabilising methods.

Looking at the future, the path to achieving full fault tolerance with advanced error correction is hard to precisely predict. Some scientists have a goal to reach one million qubits by the end of 2030, allowing this technology to advance from a proof of concept to a behemoth able to tackle large industrial-scale challenges in any and all industries.

The possession of advanced capabilities in quantum computing is concentrated in a few technological powers. Over $30 billion in public investment has been announced globally since 2016; more than 60% is attributable to China and the United States. Processors with over 100 qubits have been demonstrated by IBM and Google; terrestrial quantum communication networks exceeding 2,000 km have been brought back to China.

A stable quantum advantage has not yet been established for general-purpose applications, but the threat to RSA-2048 encryption is believed to be real in the medium term. It is estimated that millions of government and financial keys can become vulnerable in a post-quantum scenario. Post-quantum cryptography standards were selected by the National Institute of Standards and Technology in 2022, indicating an ongoing systemic transition.

In countries without advanced HPC infrastructure and semiconductor ecosystems, structural technology dependencies are being strengthened. Control of cryogenic supply chains, superconducting materials, and advanced lithography systems is concentrated in a few OECD economies. Information and security asymmetries are likely to be amplified, with effects on deterrence, intelligence, and military superiority.

The European Union is a global leader in quantum-technology research, with very strong publication output and coordinated public programmes such as the Quantum Flagship. This is one of the few sectors in which the EU remains competitive with the US and China, according to the Draghi Report (only research). Yet Europe still lags in private capital, scale-ups, and industrial deployment, and can become prey for foreign industries.

### SUMMARY

Quantum computing uses superposition and entanglement for exponential processing power, but remains at the prototype stage due to error-correction challenges. Global investment exceeds $30 billion, led by the US and China, while the EU leads in research but lags in commercialization. The looming threat to current encryption standards demands urgent adoption of post-quantum cryptography.
