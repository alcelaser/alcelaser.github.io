## **Violence**

In his treatise *Violence*, Slavoj Žižek asks us to look beyond "subjective violence"; this is the violence most people recognize immediately, the visible physical disruptions of the peaceful state of things\[^1]. The point is to instead recognize "objective violence." Objective violence is the invisible, systemic violence inherent in the "normal" state of things, the ‘base-level’ violence we accept when we participate in a system. An example of objective violence is the globalized system of labor exploitation and poverty within the supply chain: neither of us is directly responsible for the treatment of workers, yet by buying specific goods, like chocolate, we consent and ultimately fund this baseline level of violence\[^2].

In this article we’ll explore all of the ways and reasons AI can and will become the leading distributor of violence if the current state of affairs is not addressed. Currently, we are witnessing a chaotic struggle for control over this machinery between nation-states and corporations, like when the U.S. Department of Defense leveraged Anthropic’s Claude during the January 2026 invasion of Venezuela; Anthropic pushed back, fighting to restrict its use in lethal or autonomous scenarios. Now all military contractors in the United States risk losing their contracts if they do not decouple from Anthropic’s systems\[^3], even though hours before, they had been the only AI system permitted within the Pentagon. Conversely, platforms like xAI's Grok actively defy government guardrails under the banner of unchecked access, complicating the state's monopoly on information, and go unchecked. AI is no longer just a tool but an autonomous geopolitical actor. The battle over its control is the defining conflict of our era.

## **Manufactured Consent - Terms of Service**

Before AI can govern economies, it must govern perception. Jean Baudrillard’s concept of *Simulacra and Simulation* warns of a world where symbols and signs replace reality. A simulacrum is an image or idea that represents a concept, but has no physical analogue\[^4].

Through the use of simulacra, one can erode singular independent thought, by regulating our attachment to these symbols. We interact with simulacra daily, often through bots on social media. Some bots can be seen explicitly manipulating public perception on social media; by confirming cognitive biases, acting as controlled opposition and manipulating facts. To illustrate this concept one can look at the Cambridge Analytica Scandal, in which data was harvested from potential voters in order to classify them and feed them different simulacra, potentially for political gain.

Today, hyper-personalized data harvesting corrals users into digital echo chambers, subtly but effectively shifting their entire worldviews without their conscious realization.

Crucially, this system relies on the illusion of choice. We willingly subject ourselves to this surveillance architecture every time we click "Agree" on Terms and Conditions. These documents are intentionally designed as impenetrable legal labyrinths, leveraging information overload to manufacture consent. We blindly sign away our digital autonomy and our raw behavioral data, feeding the very algorithms used to manipulate us.

We are no longer governed by the physical confinement of Foucault’s disciplinary institutions\[^5]. Instead, control is a free-floating, continuous modulation. We are micro-managed by passwords, algorithms, and predatory user agreements, willingly participating in our own subjugation because the invisible walls of our echo chambers are built out of our own data.

## **Labor and the New Means of Information**

If the industrial revolution was a battle over the means of production, the AI revolution is a battle over the means of information. Geopolitically, the nation that commands the best frontier models and the most high quality data holds far more power than once was true. But domestically, this shift threatens the foundation of the working class. We are careening toward a post-labor world, yet our political structures can only imagine AI as a tool to maximize corporate efficiency, rather than liberate human time. Unsurprising, as the entire history of capitalist technological advancement has focused on maximizing profit rather than utility.

Is AI capital, or is it labor? Currently, it functions as hyper-capital: machinery that learns and executes like labor but requires no wages, sleep, or solidarity. Recent futurist modeling\[^6] has identified this as a macroeconomic paradox called “Ghost GDP”. As corporations replace white-collar jobs with AI to widen margins, GDP will continue to soar thanks to the productivity of the machines, but the velocity of money flatlines. Machines produce, but do not consume in the same way we consumers do: they do not buy houses; they do not go on vacation; and they do not pay income tax. Without intervention to redistribute the capital gained from this increase in productivity, we risk entering the “Human Intelligence Displacement Spiral”: a negative feedback loop with no natural break in which companies lay off workers to cut costs, those displaced workers then lose their spending power, leading to a reduction in demand. This reduction in demand then in turn puts more pressure onto companies, from this pressure they might start cutting costs by adopting *more* AI.

In the end all we’re left with is neo-feudalism, where the economy follows two strict paths; on one side we can see the owners of capital, with their closed loop of automated production generating immense wealth. On the other, we see a displaced populace whose labour value asymptotically reaches zero. By focusing solely on profit rather than utility power will consolidate within those who control the means of intelligence, leaving those who were once the means of production, the workers, to face systemic abandonment.

## **Capital and Commoditized Intelligence**

As AI fundamentally alters labor, it also breaks the logic of capital markets. How will algorithmic trading evolve when intelligence is no longer a human trait, but a readily purchasable, infinitely scalable commodity accessible via API? The stock market relies on information asymmetry and human speculation; a market entirely dictated by interacting superintelligences ceases to be an open market and becomes a deterministic simulation. Currently, AI acts as a massive gravitational 'drag' on the stock market, pulling capital toward the few mega-corporations that own the best models, while serving as a glaring warning sign for legacy companies destined for obsolescence. When it comes to the stock market, will the winning players be determined by model weights? Or can a stock market reliably exist when the best financial advisor you can think of lives in your pocket 24/7?

While all the attention is fully on high-paying, hard to replace sectors of the economy, labor that maximizes human utility, such as manual household labor, remains largely untouched. The trajectory of AI development perfectly maps onto this bleak reality. Billions of dollars are poured into automating cognitive labor (writers, coders and analysts) due to the immense surplus value to be extracted by replacing a salaried workforce with a server farm.

By this logic there is no direct profit in automating the folding of laundry or the sweeping of floors as human utility has no direct economic value. If AI entirely replaces labor in a system that requires individuals to sell their labor to survive, the result is not a post-work utopia, but absolute systemic abandonment. We are forced to ask: when human labor no longer brings profit, what value does the system assign to human life? What will work even mean?

But the violence of AI in capital markets extends beyond trading algorithms; it targets the very structure of the modern service economy: Intermediation. For the last fifty years, the global economy has built a massive "rent-extraction layer" on top of human friction. We pay fees for insurance brokers, travel agents, and payment processors because we are too busy or too lazy to optimize every transaction ourselves. AI agents, however, face no such friction. They can instantly re-shop insurance policies, route payments through stablecoins to avoid credit card fees, and optimize supply chains in real-time. This sounds efficient, but it represents a violent destruction of the "moats of friction" that sustain trillions of dollars in enterprise value. When AI eliminates the inefficiency, it also eliminates the revenue streams of the companies that monetized that inefficiency, all by design as we are told that an efficient financial system is one that works.\[^7] This efficiency, as it stands, is Violence.

From this logic we can conclude that our current moral framework is entirely financial. We exist in a system where justice is perpetually deferred to protect elite power structures (as glaringly exposed by the Epstein case), where military interventions are only launched if they align with corporate interests, and where systemic violence and genocide are tolerated, or even actively funded, so long as they remain highly profitable. If the entities building superintelligence operate under this strictly financial morality, we are not going to align AI with human ethics. AI will simply optimize and enforce the sociopathic, profit-driven ethics of late-stage capitalism.

## **Intelligence Dependency and the Death of Ethics**

If we lose our economic agency, we also risk losing our political and cognitive agency. Voting, the bedrock of democratic society, is increasingly endangered, reduced to a simulated choice between algorithmically managed factions and controlled opposition.

As technological dependency evolves into *intelligence dependency*, populations risk losing the capacity for critical thought, outsourcing complex decision-making to corporate-owned black boxes. Cognitive surrender in this system is no accident; it’s highly strategic. Modern populism actively thrives on this dynamic, relying on mass ignorance, anti-intellectualism, and the aggressive denial of obvious, material realities; such as climate change. Modern left-wing movements also suffer, as unity is replaced by identity politics; by picking at the right sensibilities, any large party can be split based on minor ideological discrepancies, leading to less overall agency and coordination. For the bad actors and tech oligarchs who control the means of AI, the death of critical thinking is a massive geopolitical advantage. It makes populations infinitely easier to manipulate.

We see this through the systemic consolidation of power in a new economic order that  some European scholars\[^8] call techno-feudalism, arguing that this system has replaced capitalism, particularly since 2008. In this new order, the "tech oligarchs" controlling the means of AI and information establish fiefdoms, not markets, where corporations like Amazon function less as capitalist entities and more as digital monopolies. These scholars argue that the vast wealth of these new "techno-feudal lords" is derived not from the actual profits of their businesses, but from massive increases in their share price, which is completely sustained by central bank money.

This economic framework entrenches a new ruling class whose power is maximized by maintaining the 'intelligence dependency' of the population and whose ultimate goal is a complete decoupling of economic value from human contribution. Crucially, this can only happen if we as Europeans are fully dependent on foreign technology.

Physically, we can already see the drawbacks from this technological acceleration; we just don’t see it for what it is. We call it "phone addiction" because we lack the vocabulary to describe the mass psychological experiment occurring at our brain stems. Like the first generations of the Industrial Revolution, we find ourselves at the forefront of a future we can’t reliably predict, with the past being rewritten as we speak. As technology metastasizes from the television to the smartphone and then to the AI, our capability to understand the long term effects of this technology is vastly outpaced by the evolution of this technology. Our language cannot evolve in time to capture it.

It’s called phone addiction, but we aren’t addicted to calling people; we’re addicted to stopping the base-line level of noise this technology instills deep within us. Whether they be personal self-esteem issues born from social media or political traps disguised as common sense, we all have a need to drown out the base-level violence we repeat to ourselves on a daily basis. This is all by design. By forcing us into ruminative loops, we won’t be able to even deliberate on the problem. And even if we reach a level of comprehension where discussion is possible, technology would’ve already soared past us. If there’s anything technological progress has shown it’s that it’s not evolving to be more useful; we currently have fewer days off than a medieval serf\[^9].

Rather, technology evolves to be less explicit, moving beyond the idea of technology in the disciplinary society, and moving towards an inseparable element of the human condition. It becomes harder to physically see, but it’s always more mentally present. It’s reached the point where many people internalize the noise, blaming themselves for the chaos in their heads, treating it as a personal failure. Rather, this baseline level of noise is our natural response to how this technology is being *mis*used; not the other way around.

## **Looking for New Weapons**

We are entering an era of absolute, invisible control. Yet, paralyzing despair is exactly what the system of capitalist realism\[^10] requires to function. To navigate the advent of superintelligence, the collapse of traditional labor, and the simulation of our political reality, we must shift our perspective. Following Sanders and Hinton, AI is absolutely not inherently evil; the number of positive use cases is endless. The issue isn’t the technology but who controls and shapes it. Control thrives on frictionless convenience. The tech oligarchs offer us intelligence on demand via API, slowly eroding our critical thinking until we reach total intelligence dependency.

A possible remedy against this information control is *radical friction*. It means intentionally choosing the difficult path, forcing yourself to discover how and why things work rather than being spoon-fed information. It means rejecting algorithmic curation, manually seeking out contradictory information, and refusing the hyper-personalized echo chambers built to sedate us.

This does not mean quitting Instagram for a month or caving in to that one conspiracy theory you hold so deeply, but rather radically shifting the way we interact with technology.

It means becoming unpredictable. Under this system of information, we are no longer "individuals"; we are "dividuals": fragmented data points, consumer profiles, and behavioral probabilities. To fight this, we must actively dis-identify with the digital avatars the system builds for us. By acting unpredictably, changing our patterns, and refusing to neatly align with the demographic boxes designed for us, we render ourselves illegible to the machine.

*There is no need to fear or hope, but only to look for new weapons.\[^11]*
